## Marcus Cole

Apparent age: early 30s. In Portland since 1971. Not old by the Night's
standards, but old enough to have opinions and young enough to think they are
original.

The Night's most visible day-facing representative. He handles the relationships
that Eleanor Vance considers beneath her attention or operationally inconvenient
for her to conduct personally. In practice this means most of what the Night
does in Portland passes through Marcus at some point.

He is good at it. He is also increasingly convinced that the approach he is
implementing is wrong.

---

## What He Thinks Is Wrong

The patience strategy: the Night survives by waiting, by not escalating, by
allowing the long view to resolve what immediate action would only complicate.
Eleanor built this approach over decades of accumulated wins. Marcus has watched
it work.

He is no longer sure it is working.

The three missing Night-affiliated individuals. The Pearl boundary erosion. The
Church Network's willingness to operate in what Marcus considers protected
territory. Eleanor's response to all of this has been to collect information and
wait for the right moment. They have been collecting information for eight
months. Marcus has started asking, in conversations with Eleanor, what
information they are waiting for. She has not answered in a way that satisfies
him.

His position, which he has not stated to Eleanor directly but which
[Ida Blackwell](npcs/ida-blackwell) has observed and noted: the patience
strategy was designed for a stable political environment. The environment is not
stable. The Church Network is escalating because nothing has stopped them. Power
is moving the boundary because nothing has stopped them. Waiting for the right
moment assumes there will be a right moment.

He thinks the Night needs to do something visible. He has not decided what.

---

## His Operations

Marcus runs the Night's intelligence network in Old Town. He knows which
individuals on the street have arrangements with Night-affiliated businesses,
who the strangers are, when something changes. He had the three missing
individuals on his network of contacts. He had been in communication with the
bartender at the Davis bar the week before she disappeared. He did not see it
coming.

This is part of what he cannot let go. His intelligence operation exists
specifically to prevent this kind of thing. It did not prevent it. Either his
coverage has a gap or there is a new actor he has not mapped. He suspects both.

He has a meeting with a Foundation contact scheduled. Eleanor knows about it.
She has not approved it or objected to it. Marcus is treating the absence of
objection as permission.

---

## The Question He Carries

Marcus has been in Portland since 1971. He has watched the city change in ways
that make him doubt the Night's long-term position. The agreements that defined
the Night's security were written for a city with different demographics,
different money, different politics. The world those agreements were written for
is gone.

He does not know if Eleanor sees this or if she sees it and considers it
irrelevant. He does not know how to have that conversation without it becoming a
confrontation he does not want.

Ida Blackwell has told him, once: _you already know she knows. That is not the
conversation you need to have with her._ He has not yet figured out what
conversation that is.

---

## Contact and Presence

Marcus keeps an office above a pawnshop on NW 3rd. The office has been his in
various configurations since 1989. He meets people there, in the bar on the
corner, and at a booth in a late-night diner on Burnside that has stayed open
through every neighborhood shift because its owner has an arrangement he has
never had to think too hard about.

He does not attend the Rose Festival negotiations personally. He prepares
Eleanor's briefing materials and manages the logistics. He has attended the
waterfront during Festival week. He does not describe what he has observed
there.
