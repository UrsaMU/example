## The Wild Roses

Portland is called the City of Roses. The name is marketing, mostly -- the
climate is genuinely good for roses, the city has extensive rose gardens, the
Festival is named for them. But the wild roses are something else.

They grow where they have not been planted. They come back where they have been
removed. They bloom on a schedule that does not exactly track the season. Nobody
tends them. Nobody asks why anymore.

---

## Where They Grow

The concentration is highest along the western edge of the city -- the boundary
zone where the urban fabric meets the base of the Tualatin Mountains. Forest
Park's lower trails are thick with them. They run along fences in Northwest
Portland and climb the older buildings in ways that building managers quietly
accommodate rather than fight.

Old Town has them on the blank walls of the older brick buildings. Not
cultivated -- the brickwork cracked and the roses found the cracks. The Night
does not remove them.

They appear, occasionally, in locations that have no apparent ecological path
for spread. A basement stairwell in a building that had no roses above it. A
stretch of the waterfront seawall. The interior of a parking structure on SW
Morrison that has been noted in the Foundation's records three times without
explanation.

---

## When They Bloom

The wild roses bloom slightly out of season. Not dramatically -- by a week,
sometimes two. They bloom harder during Festival week, which has been documented
since the Foundation started keeping records in the 1940s. The connection
between the Festival's ritual attention and the bloom intensity is one of the
more consistent observations in the Archive.

They do not bloom at all in the immediate vicinity of certain spaces. The
underground sections of Old Town where the Night has maintained presence the
longest are notable for their absence. The roses stop at the doorway of St.
Michael's, the auto dealership church in St. Johns, and do not grow within fifty
feet of the building.

The Foundation's Archive notes this pattern but does not attempt to explain it.
The annotation, written in what appears to be 1971 handwriting: _directional
aversion consistent with prior entries._ The prior entries are in the
secondary-lock section.

## What Happens When They're Cut

Cutting wild roses in Portland is technically unremarkable. Gardeners do it.
City maintenance crews do it. Rose Festival parade floats require enormous
quantities of cut flowers, most of them cultivated, but wild-gathered roses have
been incorporated historically.

The thing that is not unremarkable: the cut wild roses do not travel well. They
wilt faster than cultivated varieties. They resist arrangements -- florists who
have worked with them describe the stems as difficult, resistant to wire and
tape in ways that seem willful. A local florist quoted in a 1998 Oregonian
lifestyle piece, probably without realizing what she was describing: _"They want
to be in their own shape. You can fight it or you can design around it."_

The deeper thing, the thing that is not discussed in lifestyle pieces: cutting
wild roses in certain locations produces a response. Not dramatic. Not
immediate. The Wild does not manifest visibly. But people who have done it
report a sustained awareness of being noticed. Not threatened. Noticed. The
feeling persists for several days and then fades without incident.

The Church Network advises against cutting wild roses. They do not explain this
advice to newer members. When asked, senior members say only: _leave those
alone._

## The Wild's Relationship to Them

The [Wild faction](factions/wild) does not claim the roses explicitly and has
not explained their nature to the other factions. What can be inferred:

The roses are not agents of the Wild -- they do not appear to act, respond, or
communicate in any legible way. They are more like a signal: the Wild's presence
or influence in a given space, persisting in visible form after the Wild itself
has withdrawn.

The Wild is sensitive to their removal in bulk. The eco-resort proposal included
a clearing plan for vegetation in the development zone. The vegetation section
of the environmental review included wild rose mapping. Rachel Voss, the project
manager, was the person who submitted the clearing plan. This is not believed to
be coincidental.

## The Archive's Records

The Foundation's Archive has a dedicated section on the wild roses, which is
notable -- most setting phenomena get a few pages in a broader category. The
rose section runs approximately 200 pages and covers documented observations
from 1943 to the present.

Three specific entries are frequently cited by the board members who have read
them:

The 1959 entry documenting a mass cutting event -- a city beautification project
that removed approximately 40% of the NW district wild rose growth. What
followed is described in the entry as _a three-week period of unusual incident
concentration._ The incidents themselves are in the secondary-lock section.

The 1988 entry on the Reed College paper. A biology PhD candidate wrote a thesis
on the wild rose growth patterns that included data she could not explain within
conventional botanical frameworks. Her dissertation advisor, a Foundation board
member, recommended revisions. The paper was not published. The candidate
transferred to a program at Oregon State the following year. The original data
is in the Archive. The candidate, now a tenured professor in Corvallis, has not
returned to the subject.

The 2009 entry, which is only one page and reads in its entirety: _Growth
observed in LOCATION REDACTED. Confirmed not cultivated. Confirmed not
previously documented at this site. Board notified. No action taken. Board
notes: the river does not forget._

The phrase _the river does not forget_ appears in three other Archive entries
with no further explanation.

## What Nobody Knows

Whether the roses are a mechanism -- something that does something -- or a
record, like the Night describes the Willamette. Whether they can be
communicated with or through. Whether removing all of them would do anything or
whether the question is unanswerable because nobody has tried.

The Reed College paper exists in the Archive. Nobody has gone back to talk to
the former candidate. The Portland Thorn doesn't know the Archive exists. The
Foundation board has discussed the roses in closed session four times in the
past decade and reached no conclusions.

The roses grow where they grow. They come back where they are removed. Nobody
asks why anymore.
