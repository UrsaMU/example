## The Willamette River

The river divides Portland east and west. It also divides something else. What,
exactly, is contested. Crossing certain bridges at certain hours means different
rules apply. Old agreements were written with the river as their spine. As
bridges multiply and the city sprawls, those agreements get harder to enforce.

The river keeps secrets. It has been doing so for a long time.

---

## The Bridges

Portland has more drawbridges than any city in the world. Each bridge is a
crossing point. Each crossing point has a history.

Burnside Bridge: The most contested crossing. Connects Old Town to the east
side. The bridge has been in continuous operation in various forms since 1894.
Something about its position relative to Old Town's power makes it the most
politically significant span. Deals have been made on this bridge. A few people
have gone into the water from it under circumstances that were not voluntary.

St. Johns Bridge: Church Network territory to the north. The gothic towers. See
[St. Johns](locations/st-johns).

Hawthorne Bridge: The oldest operating vertical-lift bridge in the US. The
east-side counterculture's crossing point. The Thorn's staff uses it daily. They
have noticed that the bridge's behavior is slightly irregular on Festival week
and have not yet written about it.

## What the River Holds

The Willamette has been Portland's industrial drain for 150 years. It is cleaner
now than it was in 1970 but that is a low bar. The cleanup revealed some things
the sediment had been covering. Most of those things are now catalogued by the
EPA. Some are not.

The Night has an interest in the river that they have never fully explained to
the other factions. When asked, they describe the river as a record. They do not
clarify what it records.

## The Festival and the River

During the Rose Festival, dragon boats race on the Willamette. The waterfront
fills with food carts and temporary stages. The fireworks reflect off the water.
One hundred thousand people stand on the banks and watch.

Something also happens on the water during Festival week, after the crowds
leave. The unhoused community does not go near the waterfront during Festival
week. When asked why, they say they have somewhere else to be.
