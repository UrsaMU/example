## The Archive

A locked room on the second floor of the Rose Festival Foundation's headquarters
at NW 19th Avenue. The building is a converted Victorian house, publicly listed
as the Foundation's administrative offices. The Archive is at the end of the
second-floor corridor. The door is not labeled. It appears to be a closet.

It is not a closet.

Faction: Power (Foundation). The Foundation's most significant asset.

---

## What's In It

Eighty years of documented incidents. The Foundation began systematic records
in 1943. Everything before that has been reconstructed from board minutes,
personal papers of early members, and materials transferred from the estates of
board members who died with active knowledge.

The Archive runs approximately 12,000 pages of primary documentation. Roughly
two-thirds of this is accessible to any board member with the standard key. The
remaining third -- the secondary-lock section -- is accessible to the board
chair and two designated members. Currently:
[Thomas Aldrich](npcs/thomas-aldrich), the Foundation's legal counsel (who has
read it but considers herself bound not to summarize it), and a retired board
member who lives in Lake Oswego and has not been in the Archive in four years.

The accessible two-thirds covers: incident reports by date and location, faction
activity documentation, the Festival negotiations record, observations on the
wild roses, the River Assessment concerns memo, and the summary files on all
known supernatural-adjacent individuals who have come to the Foundation's
attention.

The secondary-lock section covers: the Vanport Flood documentation, the 1959
beautification incident consequences, the FESTIVAL / RIVER materials, and a set
of files labeled PRIOR AGREEMENTS that nobody currently on the board has read in
full.

## Who Has Access

Board members with the primary key: seven currently. Not all of them have used
it. Three have read the accessible section. One read the first fifty pages in
2018 and has not returned. [Priya Shetty](npcs/priya-shetty) does not have the
key. She has asked about the room twice. She has been told it is storage under
legal review.

The secondary key: the mechanism is not a standard lock. The Foundation's legal
counsel, who prepared the documentation when Aldrich took the chair, has
described it in her private notes as "a combination of physical mechanism and
something I cannot formally advise on." She has not elaborated.

[Eleanor Vance](npcs/eleanor-vance) knows the Archive exists. The Night's
position on the Archive is that it should not be destroyed but that the
Foundation's habit of not sharing it is a problem. Eleanor has said this,
through channels, several times. The Foundation has acknowledged the position
and not changed the policy.

[Ida Blackwell](npcs/ida-blackwell) has been in the Archive. How she entered is
not documented. She left a note. The note is in the accessible section. It
reads, in full: _you have more than you think and less than you need. The 1907
minutes are incomplete. The original terms are in the property record for 4421
NW Johnson, filed March 1906._ The Foundation's board verified the property
record. It exists. It has not been publicly discussed.

The Portland Thorn does not know the Archive exists.

## The Building

The Foundation's NW 19th Avenue headquarters is a three-story house built
in 1893. It has been the Foundation's office since 1912. The neighborhood around
it is expensive residential; the building is slightly anomalous in context,
which is managed by a well-maintained exterior and a plaque by the front door
that describes it as a historic civic organization.

The building's basement is not in the Archive. The basement is used for Festival
logistics storage -- float materials, parade documentation, catering equipment
contracts. It smells like dried flowers and old cardboard. It is exactly what it
appears to be.

The second floor is offices. The third floor has not been in regular use since
the 1980s. Board members who have been upstairs describe it as storage and
describe having the urge to leave quickly. Nobody has investigated this urge.
The Archive is on the second floor, not the third.

## The Thorn Source

The Foundation's current leak is a staff member, not a board member. They have
been passing documents to the Portland Thorn for eight months. The documents
they have access to are from the accessible -- not the secondary-lock --
section. They have been specifically selecting materials pointing toward the
disappearance pattern.

Why this staff member has done this, and what they know or suspect, is not fully
clear. They have not communicated their reasoning to their contact at the Thorn.
They have communicated urgency.

Thomas Aldrich has narrowed the leak to one of three individuals. He has not yet
confronted any of them. He is deciding whether confrontation or management is
the correct approach. This decision is taking longer than his previous decisions
of this type. He is aware that the delay is unusual for him. He has not examined
why.
