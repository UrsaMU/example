## Hawthorne

Southeast Portland's main counterculture corridor. Bookstores, vintage shops,
coffee roasters, tarot readers, a cooperative grocery that has been collectively
governed and collectively contentious since 1971. The kind of neighborhood that
has been "gentrifying" for twenty years without ever quite losing the texture
that makes it a target.

Faction: Contested. Mortality (Press, Church Network) and Wild influences
overlap here.

---

## The Surface

Hawthorne Boulevard runs east from the river through a dense residential grid.
The commercial strip between 20th and 39th is what people mean when they say
Hawthorne: the bookstores, the bars, the record shops, the vintage clothing. It
is Portland's most photogenic neighborhood and the one most frequently used to
illustrate the city's character in travel writing.

The travel writing is not wrong. It is also not complete.

---

## What's Underneath

Hawthorne's counterculture has always had a serious occult strand -- not the
Instagram variety, but people who have been practicing seriously for decades.
Some of them know something real. A few of them know too much. There is a
reading group that meets in the back room of a used bookstore on SE 37th that
has been meeting, under various names, since 1983. Their current reading list is
not available to the public.

The Wild's presence is weaker here than in Forest Park but not absent. The rose
bushes on residential fences in Hawthorne are denser and stranger than elsewhere
on the east side. A botanist at Reed College wrote a paper about this in 2019.
It was not published.

## The Portland Thorn

The Thorn's offices are in a second-floor walkup on SE Hawthorne. The staff is
small -- four full-time, two part-time, one editor who works from home and
nobody has met in person for three years. The office is cluttered with boxes of
documents. The door to the back room is always locked. The reporter working the
disappearances story has been in that back room every night this week.
