## Wild

Forest Park. The Tualatin Mountains. The things that were here before the city
was a concept. They are patient in ways that are difficult to comprehend. The
city has grown around them, not over them. They have opinions about the
difference.

Forest Park is the largest urban forest in the United States -- over 5,000 acres
on the ridge above the west side of the city. It is not managed. It manages
itself. The trails that appear on official maps are not the trails that matter.

---

## What the Wild Is

Not a unified faction in the sense that Power or Night are. More like a pressure
-- a set of interests and entities that converge when something threatens the
boundaries they care about. Individual members of the Wild faction may be very
old, deeply strange, and not especially legible by human standards.

They do not attend meetings. They send representatives when they choose to. The
representatives are not always comfortable to be near.

---

## The Forest Park Boundary

The city ends at Burnside to the south and at the park's eastern edge. Those are
the official boundaries. The real boundary is negotiated and has been for as
long as the city has existed. The Wild does not patrol it. It simply is.
Development that crosses it has a way of failing quietly, expensively, and
sometimes in ways that are difficult to explain to insurers.

The proposed eco-resort is the most serious boundary challenge in decades. It
has been in city planning for three years. It has not moved. The project manager
disappeared eight months ago. The project's lead investor has recently become
very interested in a different development site.

---

## The Roses

The wild roses that grow throughout Portland are, in some meaningful sense, the
Wild's presence in the parts of the city they no longer physically occupy. What
this means practically is a matter of ongoing dispute among the other factions.
The Wild does not clarify.
